*Google Cybersecurity Certificate – Course 05: Detection and Response*
*(Portfolio Activity – Document an Incident with an Incident Handler's Journal)*

---

## 🎯 Lab Summary

In this activity, I documented a cybersecurity incident using an **Incident Handler's Journal**, practicing structured incident documentation as part of the incident response process.

The main tasks were to:
- Review the details of a real-world ransomware incident scenario
- Record a complete journal entry using the 5 W's framework
- Reflect critically on the incident, including response decisions and preventive measures

---

## ⚙️ Scenario Overview

A small U.S. health care clinic experienced a security incident on a Tuesday at approximately 9:00 a.m., resulting in disrupted business operations. An organized group of unethical hackers gained network access via phishing emails containing malicious attachments. Once the attachments were downloaded, ransomware was deployed that encrypted critical files across the organization's systems. The attackers left a ransom note demanding a large sum of money in exchange for the decryption key.

## 🧠 Reflections / Notes

- This activity reinforced the importance of **accurate and timely documentation** in incident response — a journal entry is only useful if it captures the right details at the right time, which is why the *Date* field reflects when the entry was written, not when the incident occurred.
- The **5 W's framework** proved to be an effective structure for breaking down a complex security incident into clear, digestible components — ensuring that no critical detail (actor, action, timing, location, or motive) is overlooked during documentation.
- Analyzing this incident highlighted how **phishing remains one of the most effective initial access vectors**, as it exploits human behavior rather than technical vulnerabilities — making security awareness training a critical control.
- The question of **whether to pay the ransom** has no straightforward answer in cybersecurity — paying does not guarantee file recovery, encourages further attacks, and may violate regulations depending on the jurisdiction and the threat actor involved.
- This scenario demonstrated that **ransomware attacks on healthcare organizations are particularly damaging**, as the encryption of critical files can directly disrupt patient care — making the availability pillar of the **CIA Triad** especially relevant in this context.

## 📚 Key Skills Demonstrated

- Application of an **Incident Handler's Journal** as a structured documentation tool within the **NIST Cybersecurity Framework (CSF)** incident response lifecycle
- Identification and documentation of the **5 W's** (Who, What, When, Where, Why) to capture the essential details of a security incident
- Analysis of a **ransomware attack vector**, tracing the full attack chain from phishing email → malicious attachment → ransomware deployment → file encryption
- Recognition of **human error and phishing** as a primary initial access vector, highlighting the importance of security awareness training as a preventive control
- Critical thinking on **incident response decision-making**, including the ethical and legal implications of ransom payment in a healthcare context
- Understanding of how the **CIA Triad** (specifically Availability) applies to ransomware incidents targeting critical sectors such as healthcare
